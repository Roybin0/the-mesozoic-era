The Triassic Period ~ 252 - 201 million years ago

The Triassic period was a time of massive change on Earth and can be split into the Early (252 - 247 million years ago), Middle (247 - 237 million years ago) and Late (237 - 201 million years ago) Triassics. It began some 252 million years ago after the worst-ever extinction event. Known as "The Great Dying", it wiped out up to 90% of all species, animals and plants alike. It's thought that it took a few million years for the Earth to recover.

During the Triassic, Earth did not have continents - instead, there was one giant landmass called Pangaea. To the west was the Panthalassic Ocean; to the east, The Tethys. Given the vast size of this super-continent, the environment varied greatly and as it recovered from The Great Dying, trees such as conifers reaching 30 metres tall grew in large forests and gave way to ferns in drier regions. As animals recovered, so did mammal-like reptiles such as the Lystrosaurus. The first amphibians evolved in freshwater regions to live alongside the existing Temnospondyls such as Mastodonsaurus (something like a modern-day crocodile), while Ichthyosaurs evolved in the seas.

At the same time, Sphenodonts appeared (seen today as the Tuatara - a species that looks like a lizard, but is from a different heritage) which would give rise to orders like Plesiosauria. While the Early Triassic was dominated mostly by mammal-like reptiles, the Middle Triassic saw a diverse range of archosaurs appear which would give way to dinosaurs, birds, crocodiles, turtles and pterosaurs.

Groups like Aetosaurs, drepanosaurs, phytosaurs and the incredible Tanystropheus were quite successful. Pterosaurs appeared during the Middle Triassic and are the earliest known vertebrates to have powered flight. By the Late Triassic, archosaurs dominated the Earth.

The change on Earth during the Triassic period was not limited to plant and animal life - the climate changed too. So much so that by 201 million years ago, the end of the Triassic period, Earth experienced another mass-extinction event and Pangea began to break up.

Pangaea - Some 250 million years ago, Earth's continents formed one large super-continent called Pangaea
Lystrosaurus - The most common animal in the Early Triassic period; it was roughly the size of a pig.
Mastodonsaurus - A type of temnospondyl that lived during the Late Triassic. It looked like a massive frog, except with a triangular head that reached up to 1.25 metres in length.
Utatsusaurus - One of the earliest ichthyosaurs which evolved during the Early Triassic period.
Plesiosaurus - A marine reptile species of Plesiosauria that lived from the Middle Triassic until the end of the Cretaceous period
Desmatosuchus - A type of aetosaur that lived during the Late Triassic period
Drepanosaurus - A tree-dwelling chameleon-like reptile from the Middle - Late Triassic period. Only one specimen has ever been found.
Rutiodon - A type of Phytosaur from the Late Triassic period. While they resemble modern-day crocodiles, they have a very different heritage.
Tanystropheus - An archosaur with a neck longer than it's body and tail combined that lived from the middle Triassic period.
Preondactylus - An early Pterosaur from the late Triassic period, they lived until the end of the Cretaceous period.

The Jurassic Period ~ 201 - 145 million years ago

Spanning 56 million years, the Jurrasic period can also be split into the Early (201 - 174 million years ago), Middle (174 - 164 million years ago) and Late (164 - 145 million years ago) Jurassics.

In the Early Jurassic, Pangaea continued to split with the northern half broken into North America and Eurasia. The southern half began to split in the Middle Jurrasic and east (Antartica, Madagascar, India and Australia) split from west (Africa and South America). New oceans were formed in the spaces between these land masses and mountains rose on the sea floor, pushing sea levels higher.

Marine life such as plesiosaurs and ichthyosaurs had survived extinction and now dominated the seas and oceans. On land, the Early Jurassic saw dinosaurs such as Dilophosaurus, Vulcanodon, Anchisaurus and Scutellosaurus thrive, while Apatosaurus, Brachiosaurus, Diplodocus, Allosaurus and Stegosaurus flourished in the Late Jurassic.

Archaeopteryx, though to be a transitional fossil between non-avian dinosaurs and birds, also evolved in the Late Jurassic.

The change on Earth during the Triassic period was not limited to plant and animal life - the climate changed too. So much so that by 201 million years ago, the end of the Triassic period, Earth experienced another mass-extinction event.

200 million years ago, at the start of the Jurassic period, Pangaea began to split.
By the late Jurassic period, new water corridors were forming between land masses, giving rise to more diverse life.
Dilophosaurus - Lived during the Early Jurassic. Studies have shown that the crests on its skull may have been larger in one sex than the other.
Vulcanodon - Found during the Early Jurassic, this is one of the earliest Sauropods; a very large qudrupedal herbivorous dinosaur with a long neck and tail, small head and massive limbs.
Anchisaurus - A herbivorous dinosaur from the Early Jurassic. It was previously known as Megadactylus and Amphisaurus.
Scutellosaurus - A small, lightly-built dinosaur that is one of the earliest representatives of the armoured dinosaurs.
Apatosaurus - One of the largest land animals that ever existed; it had an average length of 23 metres.
Brachiosaurus - One of the most famous dinosaurs and for a long time, the largest dinosaur known. It's forelimbs were longer than it's hindlimbs, giving it a steeply inclined neck.
Diplodocus - Another well-known sauropod, Diplodocus lived alongside predators like Allosaurus in the late Jurassic period.
Allosaurus - A large predator with a big skull and dozens of sharp teeth from the Late Jurassic.
Stegosaurus - Very famous dinosaur from the Late Jurassic. It had plates down its back andlong spikes on its tail.
Archaeopteryx - A bird-like creature similar in size to amagpie, though with more in common with dinosaurs than birds. Archaeopteryx is thought to be a transitional fossil between non-avian dinosaurs and birds.
